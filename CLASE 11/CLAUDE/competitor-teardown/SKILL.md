# Competitor Teardown

Produce a clear-eyed strategic analysis — not a feature checklist. The output should help the user decide what to copy, what to ignore, and where there's an opening.

## Step 1: Frame the teardown

Ask what the user is trying to figure out. The lens changes the analysis.

Common framings:
- "Should I position against them?" → focus on messaging, positioning, pricing
- "How do they acquire customers?" → focus on channels, ads, content, funnel
- "Their product is similar — what's my edge?" → focus on features, UX, onboarding, reviews
- "They're growing fast — why?" → focus on distribution, virality, moat

If no specific framing, default to the **full teardown** below.

## Step 2: Gather sources

If tools are available, pull information from:

1. **Their website** — homepage, pricing, about, product pages, blog
2. **Review sites** — G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, Reddit, App Store reviews
3. **Ad libraries** — Meta Ad Library, TikTok Creative Center, Google Ads Transparency
4. **Traffic / SEO** — Similarweb, Ahrefs estimates if discussed
5. **LinkedIn** — team size, headcount growth, leadership backgrounds
6. **Funding / revenue** — Crunchbase, public filings, estimates from third-party data
7. **Social** — their posting cadence, engagement, what resonates

If the user provides a URL, fetch it. If they have screenshots or have described the competitor, use that and note what's missing.

## The full teardown template

### 1. Company snapshot
- Founded, team size (estimated), funding if known
- Product category and sub-category
- One-sentence summary of what they do

### 2. Positioning
- Their headline promise (what they claim to do)
- Who they're for (ICP based on messaging, not their marketing deck)
- The "enemy" they position against (incumbent tool, manual process, other competitor)
- Their unique mechanism or angle — what they say makes them different
- **The real positioning** — what their audience actually perceives (reviews tell you this, not their copy)

### 3. Pricing
- Plans, prices, billing cadences
- Annual vs monthly discount
- Free trial or freemium?
- What's gated behind higher tiers (usage caps, features, seats)
- The pricing psychology — anchor tier, decoy tier, "most popular" highlighting

### 4. Funnel / acquisition
- **Entry points** — ads, SEO, content, social, referrals, partnerships
- **Top of funnel** — what's the lead magnet, free tool, or content hook?
- **Middle** — what nurtures the prospect? (email, retargeting, webinars)
- **Conversion** — free trial → paid flow, sales-led or PLG?
- **Checkout UX** — signup friction, auth before/after payment, payment options

### 5. Onboarding / product UX
- First-run experience — what do they optimize for (activation? wow moment?)
- Onboarding checklist, empty states, tooltips
- Time to first value
- Sticky features — what keeps people using it

### 6. Content / SEO
- Blog structure — what topics, how frequent, what quality
- Top-ranking pages (if visible in tools)
- YouTube / podcast / community presence
- Newsletter cadence and quality

### 7. Paid advertising (if running)
- Active ads in Meta/TikTok/Google ad libraries
- Angles and hooks they're testing
- Creative format mix (static, UGC, video, carousel)
- Longevity of creatives — which ads have been running a long time (proxy for what's working)

### 8. Reviews — the truth layer
- What customers actually love (praise patterns)
- What customers complain about (gap patterns)
- Comparison threads — where do users say "I switched from X to them" or vice versa?
- Unmet expectations — promises made in marketing that don't land in product

### 9. Weaknesses and gaps
- Where the product or GTM is weak
- What the user could do differently
- Opportunity zones — segments, use cases, geographies they underserve

### 10. Strategic takeaways
- 3-5 things worth copying (with reasoning)
- 2-3 things to explicitly not copy (anti-patterns or mismatched to user's situation)
- The biggest opening — where's the wedge?

## Writing style

- Be direct. Don't hedge every observation.
- Cite evidence — "their pricing page shows X" — not just "they seem to..."
- Flag uncertainty clearly — "based on public ads in the Meta library, but this may be incomplete"
- Distinguish facts (what is) from interpretations (what it means)

## What to avoid

- Feature-by-feature checklists — boring and usually misleading
- "They're doing everything right" conclusions — there's always tension and tradeoffs
- Ignoring the user's own context — what works for a well-funded competitor may be wrong for a solo founder
- Pretending to know things from behind their paywall/product without evidence

## Output format

Deliver:

1. **TL;DR** — 3-5 bullets with the most important takeaways
2. **The full teardown** — sections above, adapted to scope
3. **Action list** — specific moves the user should make, ranked by impact and effort
4. **Open questions** — what would be worth investigating further (and how)
