# YouTube Script Writer

YouTube is a retention game. Every second, a viewer decides whether to keep watching. A good script earns the next 30 seconds, then the next, then the next. The hook doesn't end at second 15 — it continues through the whole video.

## Step 1: Define the video

Ask or establish:

1. **Format** — long-form talking head, tutorial/how-to, essay/documentary, vlog, listicle, Shorts?
2. **Target length** — 60s Short, 8-12 min standard, 15-30 min deep-dive?
3. **Topic and angle** — what's the video about, and what's the unique take?
4. **Channel context** — who's the audience, what do they expect?
5. **Goal** — views, subs, leads, sales? (affects CTA placement and type)

## Long-form script structure (8-12 min)

### Cold open / hook (0:00 - 0:15)
The most important 15 seconds. You have three jobs:

1. Promise a specific payoff
2. Create curiosity about how you'll deliver
3. Qualify the viewer (so the wrong audience clicks away)

Hook patterns:
- **Result upfront:** "I made $47K in 30 days from this one strategy. Here's how it actually worked."
- **Pattern interrupt:** "Everything you've been told about [topic] is wrong. Here's why."
- **Curiosity gap:** "What happens when you do [X] for 30 days? I tried it — the results surprised me."
- **Specific stake:** "If you've been [relatable pain], this video will save you [specific thing]."

Avoid:
- "Hey guys, welcome back to the channel"
- Logo animations at the start (lose the viewer in the first 3 seconds)
- Telling them what you'll tell them before telling them

### Re-hook / stakes (0:15 - 0:45)
After the initial hook, the viewer needs a reason to stay. Options:

- Clarify what they'll learn and why it matters to them specifically
- Tease something surprising coming later in the video
- Introduce yourself briefly (5-10 seconds max) — only if credibility is relevant

### Body (0:45 - [last 60s])
The substance. Rules:

- **Open loops** — tease something coming ("I'll show you the #1 mistake in a minute — it cost me $5K")
- **Pattern breaks every 30-45 seconds** — change location, b-roll shift, zoom, graphic, cut to example
- **Concrete examples** — every abstract point needs a specific example
- **Avoid filler** — "so what we're going to do is," "as I mentioned earlier"
- **Chaptering** — break the body into clear sections the editor can label

Structure options for the body:

- **Listicle:** Numbered points, each with setup → example → takeaway
- **Story arc:** Setup → conflict → resolution → lesson
- **Tutorial:** Overview → step-by-step → common mistakes → result
- **Essay:** Claim → evidence → counterargument → synthesis

### Close + CTA (last 30-60s)
- Deliver the payoff promised in the hook (if you teased something)
- One clear CTA — not three
- If the video is long, recap the key takeaway
- Ask for the specific action (sub, comment, watch next)

## Hook anatomy (applies to long-form and Shorts)

A strong hook has three beats in rapid succession:

1. **Pattern interrupt** — something that stops the scroll or slows the skip finger
2. **Promise** — what they'll get by watching
3. **Tension** — a reason to keep watching instead of leaving satisfied

Example:
> "I spent $10,000 testing Facebook ads so you don't have to [pattern interrupt]. I'll show you the 3 angles that actually worked [promise]. The last one I would never have tried — and it's now my best performer [tension]."

## YouTube Shorts (60s or less)

Different rules:

- **First 1-2 seconds are everything** — it's a swipe decision, not a click decision
- **Text overlay matters** — many watch muted
- **One idea per Short** — don't try to pack a full video into 60 seconds
- **Hook = loop** — the opening line should also be the closing line so it loops cleanly
- **CTA:** keep minimal — "Follow for part 2" or "Comment [X]"

Shorts structure:
1. 0-2s: Hook (visual + text overlay + spoken)
2. 2-45s: Substance — one clear idea, 1-2 examples
3. 45-60s: Payoff + CTA / loop

## Retention hacks that actually work

- **Name the timestamp before shifting:** "Alright, part 3 is the one that surprised me" — prepares the brain to keep going
- **Bait loops:** "But first — the mistake most people make. I'll show you at 4:30."
- **Use "you" constantly** — draw them in
- **Ask rhetorical questions** — re-engages attention
- **Concrete > abstract** — "your landing page headline" beats "your messaging"
- **Vary sentence length** — staccato short sentences inside longer ones

## What to avoid

- Over-explaining at the start ("In this video, I'm going to talk about...")
- Dropping names/jargon without defining them
- "Let me know in the comments" as the only CTA
- Monologue without b-roll, examples, or pattern breaks
- Ending on "so yeah, that's about it"
- Clickbait you can't deliver on — tanks retention worse than a weak hook

## Output format

Deliver a script formatted for production. Use timestamps. Include delivery notes where useful.

```
[0:00 — COLD OPEN / HOOK]
(B-roll: [suggestion]. On-screen text: [suggestion])

[Spoken line 1]
[Spoken line 2]

[0:15 — RE-HOOK / STAKES]

[Spoken content]

[0:45 — SECTION 1: (name)]
...

[End — CTA]

[Spoken CTA]
End card / next video suggestion: [X]
```

Also deliver, separately:

1. **Title options** — 3 title variants (all under 60 chars, click-worthy without being clickbait)
2. **Thumbnail concept** — one suggestion for the thumbnail image + overlay text
3. **Description draft** — first 150 chars for the preview, then fuller description
4. **Chapter markers** — timestamped, based on the script
5. **Pinned comment suggestion** — often a good spot for a secondary CTA
